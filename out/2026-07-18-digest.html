AI chips are now collateral

General Compute reportedly landed a $400 million Upper90 loan secured by inference chips—a neat symbol of how AI infrastructure is becoming financeable hardware, not just hype.

The details here are sparse, but the framing is striking: General Compute has secured a $400 million loan from Upper90 in what AI Insider calls the first deal backed by AI inference chips. Mezha describes the same transaction more plainly as a loan “secured by inference chips.” Either way, the takeaway is less about one borrower than about a maturing capital stack around AI compute. Inference chips—the hardware used to run models after training—are being treated as assets sturdy enough to underwrite serious debt.

Why it matters: If lenders get comfortable valuing deployed AI hardware, startups may be able to finance compute without relying only on equity rounds or hyperscaler partnerships. That could reshape who can afford to compete in AI services.

Kimi K3 joins the trillion-parameter arms race

Moonshot AI says its new 2.8 trillion-parameter model is its most capable yet, with open weights promised soon and benchmarks that invite both awe and skepticism.

Chinese AI lab Moonshot AI announced Kimi K3, described as its “most capable model to date” and clocking in at 2.8 trillion parameters. Simon Willison notes that Moonshot is calling it the first “open 3T-class model”—rounding up, a little cheekily, from 2.8T—and positioning it ahead of DeepSeek’s 1.6T v4 Pro. K3 is already available through Moonshot’s website and API, with an open-weight release promised by July 27, 2026. Moonshot’s self-reported benchmarks put K3 mostly ahead of Claude Opus 4.8 max and GPT-5.5 high, though behind Claude Fable 5 and GPT-5.6 Sol. Willison’s piece also uses the model as a way into the enduring usefulness of quirky evaluation tests like the “pelican benchmark.”

Why it matters: The race is no longer just closed frontier labs versus everyone else. If Moonshot follows through on open weights, very large models may keep migrating into broader developer ecosystems—though self-reported benchmark claims still deserve a raised eyebrow.

Sources: Simon Willison

The headline finding is stark: according to a New York Times investigation, the Trump administration has lost or abandoned hundreds of criminal cases involving protesters and immigrants charged with assaulting ICE agents. The excerpt does not spell out why the cases are crumbling—whether because of weak evidence, overcharging, procedural problems, or shifting prosecutorial choices—but the scale alone suggests a gap between public claims of threats to officers and what survives in court.

The World Cup final becomes a prediction-market stress test

Kalshi and Polymarket reportedly saw billions in bets around the soccer tournament, pushing prediction markets toward gambling-event scale.

The World Cup final has apparently done what major elections and crypto frenzies only previewed: turned prediction markets into a mass betting venue. The New York Times reports that the tournament helped Kalshi and Polymarket reach new user records, with billions in bets flowing through the platforms. The Times frames it as potentially the largest gambling event in history—a big claim, but one that captures the shift from niche forecasting culture to mainstream wagering behavior.

Why it matters: Prediction markets like to present themselves as information engines. A World Cup-driven betting surge makes them look more like consumer gambling platforms—and will likely sharpen questions about regulation, addiction, market integrity, and where “forecasting” ends.

Sources: NYT Business
