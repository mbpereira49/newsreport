AI’s data-center boom is moving onto the bond market

Oracle and other big tech firms are looking beyond cash and equity to fund the next wave of compute buildout.

The AI buildout is no longer just a capex story; it is becoming a credit story. The Times reports that large tech companies, including Oracle, need to tap bond markets for “staggering sums” to finance data centers. The phrasing matters: debt investors are being asked to underwrite the infrastructure layer of AI at a scale big enough to raise risk concerns. Oracle gets singled out not just as a tech borrower, but as the “ultimate financial source” of the Ellison media empire, making its balance-sheet choices feel less like back-office finance and more like a hinge for several ambitions at once.

Why it matters: AI enthusiasm has mostly been priced through stocks. If the boom increasingly depends on bonds, the pressure shifts to credit markets—and to the question of whether future AI revenue can justify today’s concrete, chips and power bills.

States step into the graduate-loan gap

Minnesota and Connecticut are expanding loan options after a GOP budget bill changed federal support.

A federal retreat from graduate lending is sending states scrambling. The Times reports that Minnesota and Connecticut are among the states expanding their loan options after a gap opened by the GOP budget bill. One official described the effort plainly: “We see it as a substitute program.” That is an unusually direct admission that state-level finance is being asked to replace a federal pathway for graduate students.

Why it matters: Graduate education is expensive enough that loan architecture shapes who can enroll. If states become stopgaps, access may depend more heavily on where students live—or where they can plausibly borrow.

Kimi K3 arrives with a very large open-model claim

Moonshot AI says its 2.8 trillion-parameter model is its strongest yet, with open weights promised by July 27.

The model is already available through Moonshot’s website and API, with an open-weight release promised by July 27, 2026. The benchmark picture comes with the usual caveat: Moonshot’s self-reported results mostly beat Claude Opus 4.8 max and GPT-5.5 high, while trailing Claude Fable 5 and GPT-5.6 Sol.

Why it matters: If the promised weights arrive, Kimi K3 would push the open-model race into a much larger parameter class. But the story is still partly provisional: availability, independent testing and the meaning of “open 3T-class” all matter.

Roger Summit, a pioneer of online search, dies at 95

The Lockheed researcher helped imagine near-instant computer search for scientific and technical literature.

Roger Summit, a research scientist at Lockheed who invented an early online search service, has died at 95. His key idea sounds ordinary now only because it won: a computer system that could search scientific and technical literature almost instantaneously. In the era before search became ambient infrastructure, that was a radical compression of time—turning specialized knowledge retrieval from a slow institutional process into something closer to conversation with a machine.

Why it matters: Search is one of the great hidden utilities of modern life. Summit’s work sits in the lineage behind today’s expectation that knowledge should be queryable immediately.

Sources: NYT Business
