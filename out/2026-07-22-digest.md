### A Saudi nuclear deal tests Washington’s comfort with enrichment

The Trump administration is expected to announce a U.S.-Saudi nuclear agreement that could allow Saudi Arabia to enrich nuclear fuel as part of a civilian energy project. That “could” is carrying a lot of weight.

Enrichment sits at the uncomfortable border between peaceful nuclear power and weapons capability. Critics in Congress and Israel are already alarmed, worried that giving Riyadh access to enrichment would shorten the technical path to a bomb even if the program is formally civilian.

The excerpt does not spell out the agreement’s safeguards, limits, inspection regime, or enforcement mechanisms. Those details matter enormously. A deal with strict verification and narrow conditions is one thing; a precedent that normalizes enrichment for Saudi Arabia is another.

**Why it matters:** Washington’s nuclear cooperation standards have long been part energy policy, part nonproliferation architecture. If the U.S. loosens its position for Saudi Arabia, it could ripple through Middle East security politics, complicate relations with Israel, and ignite a fight with lawmakers who see enrichment as the hard line not to blur.

## Money, Machines, and the New Center of Gravity

Artificial intelligence is no longer just a technology story. It is becoming a market story, an investment story, and, increasingly, an economic story.

A report says investment in A.I. and related companies is lifting stocks and helping drive spending across the economy. That is the bright side: a major technology cycle attracting capital, pushing corporate strategy, and giving markets a narrative they badly like to have.

The excerpt does not quantify the boom, so the scale remains unspecified here. But the framing is clear enough: A.I. is acting as stimulus and stress test at once.

**Why it matters:** When a single technology narrative supports stock prices, capital spending, and corporate planning, it becomes macroeconomic infrastructure. That can be powerful. It can also make the system more brittle if too many assumptions depend on the same future arriving on schedule.

### A Mac app makes local A.I. feel less like a hobby project

Nativ, a new macOS desktop app for running A.I. models locally, points to a quieter but important shift: local models are getting friendlier.

Highlighted by Simon Willison, the app comes from Prince Canuma, developer of the MLX-VLM Python library for running vision-language models with Apple’s MLX framework. Nativ wraps that ecosystem in a desktop interface shaped somewhat like LM Studio, with a chat UI and a localhost API server.

The small detail that says a lot: Willison notes that Nativ detected MLX models already sitting in his Hugging Face cache. That kind of quality-of-life feature is not glamorous, but it is exactly how developer tools cross from “interesting weekend project” into “thing I might actually keep using.”

This is not about a new model. It is about the layer between open models and ordinary workflows getting smoother.

**Why it matters:** Local LLMs are moving out of the command-line clubhouse. If tools like Nativ keep improving, more developers will be able to prototype privately, offline, and cheaply on machines they already own. That matters for experimentation—and for anyone wary of sending every prompt to a cloud service.  
_Source: [Simon Willison](https://simonwillison.net/2026/Jul/21/nativ/)_

## The Serendipity Desk

### New York’s bats appear to be making a comeback

New York may be getting more bats.

After disease reduced their numbers years ago, the city’s bat population now appears to be rising. The language is cautious—“looks as though,” not “definitively has”—so this is not being presented as a final census or sweeping ecological recovery. But it is a welcome urban-wildlife note all the same.

Bats tend to be treated as seasonal décor, horror shorthand, or background flickers at dusk. In reality, they are part of the city’s living infrastructure: small, nocturnal, easy to overlook, and more at home in urban space than many people assume.

**Why it matters:** A bat rebound would suggest that even dense cities can support recovering wildlife populations when disease pressure eases and habitat remains usable. It is also a useful correction to the idea that urban nature only means parks, pigeons, and street trees. Sometimes the city’s health is visible only after sunset.
