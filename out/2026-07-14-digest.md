## The Lead: Inflation Relief, Policy Fog

### Inflation cools during pause in war with Iran

June brought the kind of inflation number that lets everyone exhale — briefly. The Consumer Price Index slowed to a **3.5% annual increase**, and prices fell month to month by the most since 2020. That is real relief for consumers, and it gives the Federal Reserve a little more room to maneuver.

But the framing matters: the slowdown came during a pause in the war with Iran, and the excerpt does not establish how much of the price move was caused by that pause versus other forces. In other words, this is good news, not yet a new regime.

**Why it matters:** one soft CPI print can change the mood; it cannot, by itself, settle the inflation argument. If geopolitics turns volatile again, the Fed may quickly find itself back in the uncomfortable business of separating temporary price relief from durable disinflation.  
_Source: [NYT Business][cpi]_

---

### Warsh keeps the Fed’s anti-inflation line — without showing his rate hand

Kevin M. Warsh is saying the central bank remains committed to bringing inflation down. What he is not saying: whether he believes higher interest rates are needed to get there.

That distinction is the whole story. The message is hawkish in destination, carefully vague on transportation. For markets, households and businesses, this means policy remains less a map than a weather report: watch the pressure systems, infer the path.

**Why it matters:** Warsh’s refusal to pre-commit preserves flexibility if inflation proves sticky. It also keeps uncertainty elevated around borrowing costs — and uncertainty itself is a kind of policy tightening when companies and consumers delay decisions.  
_Source: [NYT Business][warsh]_

## AI Leaves the Screen

### New York hits pause on the biggest data centers

New York is preparing to become the first state with a statewide moratorium on large data centers. Gov. Kathy Hochul plans to sign an executive order pausing construction of the largest projects for one year while officials study the industry’s energy and environmental costs.

This is a blunt move aimed at a fast-growing sector. Data centers used to sit in the background of the internet, physically present but politically quiet. AI has changed that. The invisible backend now looks a lot like a visible grid problem.

**Why it matters:** the data-center boom is where AI demand meets electricity reality. New York’s pause could become a template for states trying to balance tech investment, power capacity and climate commitments — especially if “build more compute” starts colliding with “where does the power come from?”  
_Source: [NYT New York Region][data-centers]_

---

### Open-source AI faces its stress test

Interconnects frames the next stretch starkly: open models may have “6 months to live,” at least in their current competitive posture. The excerpt does not lay out every pressure behind that claim, but the underlying worry is clear enough: open AI may be falling behind closed, better-funded, more tightly controlled systems.

That is more than a developer-community anxiety. Open models shape who can experiment, who can build companies without renting the entire stack from a few providers, and how widely frontier-like capabilities can be studied.

**Why it matters:** if open models lose pace, AI consolidation could accelerate. Research access narrows, startup strategy changes, and the center of gravity shifts toward closed platforms that control the model, the tooling and the distribution.  
_Source: [Interconnects][open-models]_

---

### Codex’s reported surge sharpens the AI coding-agent race

Latent Space reports that Codex usage has risen more than tenfold in six months to **7 million users**, including **1 million added in roughly the past day**. The post asks whether Codex has overtaken Claude Code — but leaves that as a question, not a verdict.

Still, the scale of the reported growth is hard to ignore. Coding agents are one of the cleanest tests of whether AI becomes daily infrastructure rather than occasional novelty. Developers either keep the tool open all day, or they do not.

**Why it matters:** if Codex’s numbers hold up, developer tooling may be shifting faster than public benchmark chatter suggests. Workflow lock-in happens quietly: first autocomplete, then delegated tasks, then an agent becomes part of how software teams think about labor.  
_Source: [Latent Space][codex]_

## Power, Law, and the New Middlemen

### Kagan warns Supreme Court threats are getting dangerously close

Justice Elena Kagan told a House panel that Supreme Court Police estimate threats are up **38% this year**. Her prepared remarks included the phrase “threats have come very close” — careful language, but not reassuring language.

The judiciary’s legitimacy crisis is usually discussed in terms of opinions, ideology and public trust. This points to something more immediate: physical risk around the institution and the people inside it.

**Why it matters:** when public fury turns into intimidation, the problem is no longer just polarization. Courts depend on legitimacy, but also on the basic ability of judges and staff to work without fear of violence. Kagan’s warning suggests that line is under strain.  
_Source: [NYT U.S.][kagan]_

---

### States muscle into the Paramount–Warner Bros. Discovery fight

The effort to block Paramount’s planned takeover of Warner Bros. Discovery is being cast as a sign of a broader shift: states are becoming more assertive antitrust players.

The excerpt does not identify which states are involved or the legal theories at issue. But the shape of the fight is familiar: a major media consolidation push meets a more fragmented enforcement landscape. The old assumption — that big national deals are mainly a federal matter — looks less sturdy.

**Why it matters:** state antitrust action can complicate the timing, economics and risk profile of major mergers. For media companies already squeezed by streaming economics and consolidation pressure, another layer of enforcement could change the deal calculus.  
_Source: [NYT Business][paramount]_

## Serendipity Desk

### The very practiced art of looking reckless

At Seattle’s International Stunt School, film and TV veterans teach performers how to turn danger into movie magic: falls, fights, fire, and the other screen moments designed to look chaotic precisely because they are controlled.

That paradox is the charm. The audience is meant to feel risk, not notice technique. But the school setting makes the labor visible — the body mechanics, rehearsal, engineering and trust that sit beneath a few seconds of apparent mayhem.

**Why it matters:** stunt work is one of cinema’s great invisible crafts. It asks performers to make discipline look like recklessness, and asks viewers to believe in danger while safely enjoying the illusion.  
_Source: [NYT Movies][stunts]_

## Closing Thought

The day’s stories keep circling the same question: who gets to manage risk?

The Fed wants inflation credibility without boxing itself in. New York wants AI infrastructure without blindly absorbing its energy costs. Open-source developers want frontier access without dependence on closed giants. Courts want public accountability without physical danger. Studios want scale; states want a say.

And somewhere in Seattle, stunt performers are practicing the cleanest version of the whole problem: make risk legible, make it controlled, and never let the audience see the wires.
