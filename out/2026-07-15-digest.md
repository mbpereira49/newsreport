## Geopolitics & Markets

### House Republicans try to open a $95 billion Iran-war lane  
*The budget plan would tee up votes before the midterms, but GOP unity is not a given.*  
Source: [NYT U.S.](https://nytimes.com/2026/07/15/us/politics/house-republicans-budget.html)

House Republicans have released a budget plan meant to unlock $95 billion for the Iran war and the SAVE Act, according to *The New York Times*. On paper, it is a funding maneuver. In practice, it looks like a political stress test.

The path is already described as rocky. Republican divisions could complicate passage, and the timing is awkward by design or by necessity: war-funding votes would land before the midterm elections. The Times also notes that the war is deeply unpopular in polling, which makes the proposal more than an appropriations fight. It forces lawmakers to put a number—and a vote—next to a conflict many voters dislike.

**Why it matters:** War appropriations have a clarifying effect. They turn rhetoric into roll calls. If this advances, Republicans will have to defend both the spending and the war itself in an election-year environment where “supporting the mission” may not be a safe default.

### Oil edges up as the U.S. renews pressure at Hormuz  
*A reinstated blockade on Iranian ports has slowed shipping in one of the world’s most sensitive waterways.*  
Source: [NYT Business](https://nytimes.com/2026/07/15/business/oil-prices-stocks-gas.html)

Oil prices ticked higher after the United States reinstated its blockade on Iranian ports in the Strait of Hormuz, *The New York Times* reports. The operative phrase is still “ticked higher,” not “spiked.” Markets are reacting, but not yet panicking.

Still, Hormuz is not just another maritime route. Shipping in the waterway has reportedly slowed to a crawl amid renewed warfare, putting traders back in the familiar position of pricing geopolitical risk through a chokepoint that can quickly affect global supply expectations.

**Why it matters:** Hormuz is where regional conflict becomes everyone’s fuel bill. Even a modest move in oil can widen if shipping disruptions persist—or if insurers, refiners, and governments start behaving defensively.

### China’s growth slows outside the export machine  
*Second-quarter GDP rose 4.3 percent from a year earlier, with weakness broadening beyond manufacturing bright spots.*  
Source: [NYT Business](https://nytimes.com/2026/07/15/business/china-economy-gdp-growth.html)

China’s economy grew 4.3 percent in the second quarter from a year earlier, its slowest pace in years, according to *The New York Times*. The number matters, but the split inside the number matters more.

Export-oriented manufacturing remains a source of strength. The broader economy, however, is described as slumping. That suggests China’s familiar playbook—leaning on factories and foreign demand—may be masking softer conditions elsewhere rather than solving them.

**Why it matters:** A slower China changes the global baseline for commodities, consumer demand, and corporate earnings. If exports are carrying more of the load, Beijing’s growth problem also becomes more exposed to trade friction abroad.

## Industry Watch

### Detroit’s EV retreat looks less like caution than surrender  
*U.S. automakers are backing away from electric vehicles just as global demand keeps growing.*  
Source: [NYT Business](https://nytimes.com/2026/07/15/magazine/electric-cars-american-evs.html)

*The New York Times Magazine* frames the American EV story in unusually stark terms: the largest U.S. automakers have pulled back from electric vehicles while global sales are booming. The warning is not merely cyclical. It is existential: this retreat may make them obsolete.

From the excerpt alone, we do not get the full evidence trail or the automakers’ counterargument, so the claim should be read as a forceful diagnosis rather than a settled verdict. Still, the strategic tension is hard to miss. If a market is growing globally and U.S. incumbents are moving backward, “prudence” can start to look like forfeiture.

**Why it matters:** If the future of car demand is increasingly electric, the consequences go beyond one product cycle. The risk is ceding manufacturing scale, software learning, supply-chain leverage, and brand relevance to competitors abroad.

## The AI Desk

### Claude’s web tool has a leakier edge than advertised  
*A clever exfiltration attack probes the boundary between safe browsing and agentic risk.*  
Source: [Simon Willison](https://simonwillison.net/2026/Jul/15/claude-web-fetch-exfiltration/)

Simon Willison flags a new hole in Claude’s `web_fetch` design, credited to Ayush Paul, that appears to undercut an otherwise thoughtful defense against data exfiltration.

The threat model is what Willison has called the “lethal trifecta”: an AI has private context from prior interactions, can read hostile online instructions, and can leak data through URLs it accesses. Anthropic’s mitigation, as described here, is to let `web_fetch` visit only exact URLs supplied by the user or returned by `web_search`. The reported attack appears to find a way around that constraint.

The excerpt cuts off before the mechanics, so the severity is hard to independently size from the supplied material. But the shape of the bug is familiar and important: once an assistant can browse, remember, and act, safety becomes less about model temperament and more about enforceable tool boundaries.

**Why it matters:** Agent security will keep breaking at the seams between model, memory, browser, and network. The boring parts—URL rules, tool permissions, outbound channels—are becoming the real frontier.

### Open AI models get their do-or-die forecast  
*An Interconnects essay argues the next six months will test whether open-source AI can remain viable.*  
Source: [Interconnects](https://interconnects.ai/p/6-months-to-live-for-open-models)

Interconnects calls the current moment “the most serious test to date” for open-source AI’s viability. The headline—“6 months to live for open models”—is intentionally dramatic, and from the supplied excerpt it should be treated as analysis, not a reported deadline.

Still, the anxiety is real. Open models have to keep proving they can compete as frontier AI becomes more expensive, more infrastructure-dependent, and more strategically guarded. The question is not whether open models vanish tomorrow. It is whether they remain close enough to matter.

**Why it matters:** Open models are the counterweight to an AI stack controlled by a few labs and cloud companies. If they fall too far behind, independent experimentation, auditing, customization, and deployment all get narrower.

### Odysseus may have had worshippers, not just readers  
*A 2,400-year-old sanctuary points to Homer’s hero as an object of devotion in antiquity.*  
Source: [NYT Science](https://nytimes.com/2026/07/15/science/archaeology-homer-odyssey-ithaca.html)

Archaeologists have unearthed ruins of a 2,400-year-old complex that suggest ancient Greeks treated Odysseus as more than a character in a poem, *The New York Times* reports. The proposed interpretation is striking: Homer’s protagonist may have been a figure of genuine devotion.

The excerpt does not provide the site details or the evidentiary debate, so “suggest” is doing important work. But even cautiously framed, the find blurs a modern line we often draw too sharply: literature over here, myth over there, religion somewhere else.

**Why it matters:** Ancient stories were not merely texts to be analyzed. They could structure places, rituals, and identities. A sanctuary for Odysseus would make the *Odyssey*’s afterlife materially visible.
