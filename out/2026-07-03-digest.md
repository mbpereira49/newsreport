# Personal Daily  
*Friday, July 3, 2026 — America/Los Angeles*

Power lost in storms, power failing at a sewage plant, power exercised through pardons, and power slowly being organized in AI’s supposedly open commons. Then, because a daily paper should leave a window open, we end with restaurants, film, and one extremely ill-advised proposal venue.

## The Lead: When the Grid Fails in the Heat

### Storms knock out power as heat bears down

A punishing weather double-hit landed across the Midwest, Northeast and Ontario: strong thunderstorm winds cut electricity to roughly 1 million customers while the region was already dealing with a heat wave. The available report is brief, but the scale is enough. A power outage is one thing; a power outage in dangerous heat is another category entirely.

Cooling is not a comfort add-on in these conditions. It is basic public-health infrastructure, especially for older residents, medically vulnerable people and anyone without a backup plan. Storm damage becomes more than storm damage when the grid goes dark at the exact moment people most need relief.

The uncomfortable lesson is familiar: extreme weather does not hit one system at a time. It stacks pressure on electricity, emergency response, health care, transportation and household resilience. A million customers without power is not just a utility metric. It is a map of risk.  
Source: [NYT U.S.][storms]

## Infrastructure Is Invisible Until It Isn’t

### Sewage spill hits the Hudson after treatment-plant failure

A power failure at a wastewater treatment plant just north of New York City sent sewage into the Hudson River from Thursday night into Friday afternoon. Officials did not expect the spill to affect New York City’s Fourth of July celebrations — a narrow reassurance, and maybe the most revealing kind.

The holiday may proceed. The river, meanwhile, absorbed the consequence of a plant losing power.

Wastewater systems are among the least glamorous forms of civilization: pipes, pumps, treatment stages, backup systems, maintenance schedules. They become visible mostly when something breaks. This spill is a reminder that environmental protection is not only about laws and limits; it is also about redundancy, funding and response time.

The report does not offer a full accounting of the failure. But the outline is enough to ask the hard question: how many critical systems are only one power failure away from becoming a public problem?  
Source: [NYT New York Region][hudson]

## Power, But the Constitutional Kind

### Trump uses pardons to undercut environmental enforcement

Trump’s latest clemency moves are being framed less as mercy at the margins than as policy by pardon. According to the report, he pardoned people who violated the Clean Air Act, further weakening environmental law enforcement, and also helped a political supporter who had pleaded guilty in a fraud case tied to Jack Abramoff.

The details in the available excerpt are limited, so the caution here is procedural: pardons are broad constitutional tools, and each case has its own record. But the signal matters. When clemency reaches environmental crimes and donor-linked fraud, it tells agencies, prosecutors and regulated industries something about what the presidency values — and what it may be willing to excuse.

Environmental enforcement depends partly on deterrence: the belief that violations carry consequences. Pardons can scramble that logic. They do not rewrite the Clean Air Act, but they can weaken the perceived seriousness of breaking it.

That is the deeper story: not just who was pardoned, but what kind of conduct is being politically softened.  
Source: [NYT U.S.][pardons]

## The AI Desk

### A first map of the open-source AI stack arrives

“Open-source AI” is one of those phrases that can mean almost anything depending on who is holding the microphone. Simon Willison flags a useful attempt to make it more concrete: Current AI’s Gap Map v0.1.

Current AI is described as a global partnership building a public option for AI, founded as a nonprofit at the Paris AI Action Summit in February 2025 and backed by $400 million in committed capital. Its new map catalogs 421 products: 266 software tools and libraries, 85 models, 50 datasets and 20 hardware projects, produced by 228 organizations.

The map organizes those projects into 14 categories across three layers of the stack: model components, product and user experience, and infrastructure. Just as interesting is what remains outside the frame: 24,400 uncategorized artifacts in the long tail.

That long tail may be the real story. Open ecosystems are often celebrated in the abstract, but they are hard to govern, fund or improve when no one can see them clearly. A map is not a movement. But it gives the movement a surface area: here are the shared components, here are the gaps, here is what is thriving, and here is what may only be called open because the label is useful.

For AI builders, policymakers and critics alike, inspectability beats vibes.  
Source: [Simon Willison][gapmap]

### AI engineers end their fair debating loops — and what comes next

The AI Engineer World’s Fair wrapped not with a single grand thesis, but with the sound of a field arguing itself into shape. Latent Space’s dispatch says the close included a “great loops” debate, a state-of-the-field report and keynotes about what builders should make next.

The excerpt does not define the loops dispute, so we should not pretend to know the contours. Still, the larger movement is clear enough: AI engineering is becoming a discipline with its own patterns, fights and professional instincts. The center of gravity is shifting from model awe to systems: architecture, workflow, evaluation, infrastructure and product judgment.

That may sound less glamorous than a demo. It is probably more important. The next wave of AI products will be shaped not just by which model is strongest, but by the practices engineers normalize around it. Loops, agents, evals and tooling may become the grammar of the field.

The fair’s ending, then, sounds less like a finale than a handoff: enough marveling; now decide how the machines should actually be built into things.  
Source: [Latent Space][aiewf]

## City, Culture, Appetite

### Jersey City gets a long-awaited Indian restaurant from a Manhattan power player

After more than 20 years living in Jersey City while building recognized Indian restaurants in Manhattan, restaurateur Jimmy Rizvi is turning attention to the South Asian community in his own backyard.

Eater reports that Rizvi — behind Gramercy’s GupShup and the popular East Village restaurant Bungalow with Vikas Khanna of *MasterChef India* — had long watched Jersey City’s large South Asian community. Now he is opening a restaurant there, in a story framed not merely as expansion but as homecoming.

Restaurant openings often get treated as lifestyle chatter, but they can be civic signals. Who gets served? Which neighborhoods are seen as destinations rather than feeder markets? What does it mean when a restaurateur who built prestige across the river finally opens for the community where he has lived for decades?

The headline’s claim is bold: this is the Indian restaurant Jersey City has been waiting for. Even allowing for restaurant-opening hype, the underlying point is compelling. Sometimes the most interesting move is not outward, but back toward home.  
Sources: [Eater NY][eater1], [Eater New York][eater2]

### Alice Winocour discusses her Angelina Jolie cancer drama

RogerEbert.com’s “Female Filmmakers in Focus” turns to Alice Winocour and *Couture*, described in the excerpt as an emotive cancer drama starring Angelina Jolie.

There is not much plot or critical detail in the supplied material, but the format matters. Director interviews can add texture beyond the review cycle, especially for films whose subject matter risks being flattened into prestige shorthand: illness, performance, awards-season seriousness, star transformation. Hearing from the filmmaker can clarify intent — not as a replacement for criticism, but as another lens.

Winocour’s conversation appears to sit at that intersection of authorship and emotion: how to tell a story about illness without reducing it to a subject, how to frame a major star inside intimate material, and how to make the craft visible without draining the drama.

A useful reminder: sometimes the best film writing is not the verdict, but the conversation after the lights come up.  
Sources: [RogerEbert.com][winocour1], [Roger Ebert][winocour2]

## The Oddly Modern Police Blotter

### Empire State Building engagement stunt ends in criminal allegations

Angela Nikolau and Ivan Beerkus, extreme climbers known for illegally scaling buildings around the world, were arrested after getting engaged atop the Empire State Building. Prosecutors accuse them of breaking a security door to reach the spire of the Manhattan landmark.

The legal picture remains preliminary based on the excerpt, which gives the allegation and arraignment context. But as an urban parable, it is almost too neat: romance, risk, trespass, skyline, viral spectacle and one very famous building that does not exist merely as a backdrop for content.

Iconic buildings carry a strange double life. They are private or managed spaces with security protocols, and they are also public symbols people feel entitled to inhabit imaginatively. Social media has made that tension sharper. The more spectacular the stunt, the more likely it is to travel — and the more likely cities and building owners are to worry about copycats.

A proposal at the top of the Empire State Building is a classic New York fantasy. Allegedly breaking through security to get there is something else.  
Source: [NYT New York Region][empire]

## Closing Thought

Today’s stories keep circling the same question: what holds? The grid under heat, a treatment plant under failure, environmental law under political pressure, open-source AI under the weight of its own ambiguity, a city’s food culture under the pull of community, and a landmark under the pressure of spectacle.

Systems are easiest to admire when they work quietly. The news begins when they don’t.
