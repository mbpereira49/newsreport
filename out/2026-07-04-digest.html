The chatbot enters the voting booth, unofficially

Voters are asking AI tools who to vote for — an appealing shortcut in civic life, but one with obvious risks.

“Who should I vote for?” is exactly the sort of question AI tools are now being asked — and exactly the sort of question they are poorly positioned to answer cleanly. The Times frames the trend as a shortcut for voters who want to be informed but face the usual time, attention and complexity problems of democratic life. That allure is real: a chatbot can digest, compare and summarize. But the risk is equally real, because elections are not just information-retrieval problems. Candidate choice involves values, trust, local context and source quality — all places where AI systems can sound more authoritative than they deserve.

Why it matters: If voters outsource political judgment to chatbots, the design choices and failure modes of those systems become part of election infrastructure, whether or not anyone voted to put them there.

Sources: NYT U.S.

A first map of the open-source AI sprawl

Current AI’s new Gap Map catalogs hundreds of tools, models, datasets and hardware projects — and hints at just how much of the ecosystem remains unclassified.

Simon Willison points to a useful attempt to impose order on open-source AI’s messy abundance. Current AI — described as a nonprofit “global partnership building a public option for AI,” founded at the Paris AI Action Summit in February 2025 and backed by $400 million in committed capital — has launched its Gap Map v0.1. The map details 421 products: 266 software tools and libraries, 85 models, 50 datasets and 20 hardware projects, produced by 228 organizations. It sorts them into 14 categories across three layers of the stack: model components, product/UX and infrastructure. The kicker is the long tail: another 24,400 artifacts remain uncategorized.

Why it matters: Open-source AI is no longer a tidy list of models on a leaderboard. Mapping the stack is becoming part of governing, funding and understanding it.

Sources: Simon Willison
