The DRI Cannot Be a Bot

Simon Willison applies an old Apple management idea to the new agent era: accountability still needs a human address.

Willison went looking for a crisp definition of “Directly Responsible Individual” and found one in GitLab’s handbook: the person “ultimately accountable for the success or failure of a specific project, initiative, or activity.” The term apparently originated at Apple, but his point is current: LLM-powered agents may do work inside organizations, yet they should not be treated as the DRI. His line is simple and useful: machines can act, but they cannot take accountability for their actions in the way humans can.

Why it matters: As companies wire agents into workflows, this is the governance question hiding under the productivity pitch: who owns the outcome when the software did the thing?
