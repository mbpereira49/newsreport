03

White-collar workers are being paid to automate themselves

Startups are hiring professionals to teach AI models how to do their jobs. The Times calls it both a bonanza and bleak, which is about right.

The Times’ framing is unusually blunt: “The Work of Helping A.I. Destroy Work.” The mechanism is straightforward and unsettling. Startups pay white-collar professionals to transfer their expertise into AI systems—turning tacit knowledge into training material, workflows, evaluations, and examples that models can absorb. For the workers, this can be lucrative now. For the labor market, it raises the obvious question: how long does the payday last if the product is a machine that can imitate more of the job? The excerpt does not specify which professions are most affected or how close the models are to replacement versus assistance, so the endgame remains contested. But the moral tension is already here.

Why it matters: This is one of the cleanest examples of AI’s labor paradox: the people best qualified to improve the systems may also be the people most exposed if those systems succeed.

Sources: NYT Business

Why it matters: If this approach works beyond one exceptional project, it hints at a practical frontier for AI in software: not replacing engineering judgment, but scaling tedious, risky migrations under tight human supervision.
