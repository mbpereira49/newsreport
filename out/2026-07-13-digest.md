## The Human on the Hook

### The DRI Cannot Be a Bot  
*Accountability still needs a human address.*  
Source: [Simon Willison][dri]

Simon Willison went looking for a clean definition of “Directly Responsible Individual” and found one in GitLab’s handbook: the person “ultimately accountable for the success or failure of a specific project, initiative, or activity.” The idea apparently traces back to Apple, but its second life belongs squarely to the agent era.

The useful point is not nostalgia for management jargon. It is the boundary line. LLM-powered agents may increasingly act inside organizations — drafting, filing, querying, purchasing, escalating, perhaps even negotiating. But they should not be treated as the DRI.

Machines can do work. They cannot take accountability in the human sense: explain themselves under pressure, absorb institutional consequences, make a values judgment, or own the fallout when the workflow does exactly what it was told and still causes harm.

**Why it matters:** The productivity pitch around agents often hides the governance question. When software “did the thing,” who owns the outcome? Willison’s answer is bracingly simple: not the bot.

### Judge Rebukes Trump I.R.S. Deal as Self-Dealing  
*A court objected sharply to an arrangement the Times describes as amnesty-like, though the immediate effect remains murky.*  
Source: [NYT U.S.][irs]

A judge denounced an I.R.S. suit as an improper exercise in self-dealing, according to the Times. The decision did not explicitly kill the deal President Trump had worked out with his own government — an arrangement the excerpt characterizes as amounting to amnesty from investigations into tax returns already filed by him, his family and their businesses.

That leaves the legal status a little unresolved. But the judicial language is not subtle. The issue is less procedural housekeeping than constitutional smell test: can a president negotiate with the government he controls over scrutiny of his own finances?

**Why it matters:** Democratic institutions often depend on boring-seeming enforcement systems. Tax review is one of them. Once personal immunity becomes negotiable from inside the executive branch, the ordinary machinery starts to look like private leverage.

### Europe Edges Toward a Children’s Social Media Ban  
*The European Commission is considering bloc-wide rule changes after a new report.*  
Source: [NYT Business][eu-social]

The European Commission is considering changes to social media rules across the 27-nation bloc, with a possible ban for children on the table. The available excerpt does not specify the age threshold, enforcement mechanism, or depth of political support, so this remains a step toward policy rather than settled law.

Still, the direction is meaningful. Europe may be preparing to treat youth access to social platforms as a regulatory problem, not merely a parental-management problem. That would shift the default burden toward platforms: design for restriction and verification first, then argue about implementation.

**Why it matters:** If the EU moves, the effects may not stay neatly inside Europe. Large platforms tend to prefer scalable compliance systems. A European children’s ban could become a global design constraint by other means.

## Land, Risk, and Rates

### Brooklyn Chooses Apartments Over a Waterfront Buffer  
*A tall waterfront development has been approved despite residents who wanted parkland for flood protection.*  
Source: [NYT New York Region][brooklyn]

The Times frames a Brooklyn decision as a stark urban trade-off: housing is winning over flood protection. A towering apartment development has been approved on the waterfront, while some residents would have preferred the site become parkland — a softer edge against future flooding.

The available excerpts do not provide the project’s precise location, scale, or mitigation measures, so the technical case is incomplete. But the civic tension is clear enough. Dense cities need homes, especially on valuable land. Those same cities also need places where water can go.

**Why it matters:** The hardest climate fights will not always be about denial versus action. Increasingly, they will pit two real public needs against each other: housing supply and physical resilience. Some parcels can serve both, but rarely equally.

### A Fed Governor Wants More Proof Inflation Is Cooling  
*Christopher Waller warned that rates could move higher if inflation stays elevated.*  
Source: [NYT Business][fed]

Federal Reserve governor Christopher J. Waller said he needs several months of lower inflation data before feeling confident about the outlook. The warning, as summarized by the Times, is that higher rates remain possible if inflation stays elevated.

It is a familiar central-bank posture, but a consequential one. One good report is not a trend. The Fed wants a pattern — repeated evidence that inflation is cooling before it blesses a looser path.

**Why it matters:** Rate-cut optimism can outrun the data. Waller’s message is that borrowing costs remain hostage to repeated evidence, not vibes. That matters for mortgages, business investment, market pricing and the political mood around the economy.

### Oil’s Next Move May Depend Less on OPEC Than on China  
*As the U.S.-Iran truce frays, China’s demand-side power looms larger.*  
Source: [NYT Business][oil]

The old oil-market story centered on OPEC and how much crude it chose to produce. This Times piece points to a shift in emphasis: China, as the world’s largest oil importer, is demonstrating remarkable power over prices.

The immediate question is whether oil prices will rise as the U.S.-Iran truce frays. But the deeper story is demand-side leverage. Producers still matter; no one has repealed supply. Yet China’s buying behavior — whether it stockpiles, pauses, redirects or accelerates demand — may be the variable to watch.

**Why it matters:** Energy shocks are geopolitical, but price formation is not just about who pumps. A country that can move demand at scale can shape the market without producing a barrel.

## Screens, Studios, Bodies

### States Sue to Block Paramount-Warner Bros. Merger  
*California and 11 other states say the deal would hurt theaters and the entertainment industry.*  
Source: [NYT Business][paramount]

California and 11 other states have challenged the proposed Paramount-Warner Bros. merger in court. Their lawsuit argues that combining the companies would harm movie theaters and damage America’s entertainment industry.

The excerpt does not include the companies’ response or the legal theory in detail, so this is an opening challenge, not a verdict. But the politics of the case are worth noticing. State antitrust enforcers are placing themselves directly in the path of media consolidation — and framing the issue not just as corporate concentration, but as ecosystem damage.

**Why it matters:** Theater economics are already fragile. If states can argue that a studio merger threatens the broader moviegoing system, entertainment deals may face a tougher antitrust lane than they once did.

### Where Movie Stunts Become a Craft, Not a Dare  
*At Seattle’s International Stunt School, veterans teach newcomers falls, fights and fire.*  
Source: [NYT Arts][stunts]

At the International Stunt School in Seattle, TV and film veterans teach the practical grammar of action: how to fall, fight and work with fire. It is a useful corrective to how stunts appear onscreen.

The point of a stunt is often to look uncontrolled. The work behind it is the opposite: repetition, safety protocols, timing, trust, and bodies trained to make danger legible without simply courting it. Movie magic, in this case, is less illusion than disciplined choreography pretending to be chaos.

**Why it matters:** A good stunt convinces the eye that something has gone wrong. The craft lies in making that illusion repeatable enough for everyone to walk away.

## One Through-Line

Today’s edition is full of systems asking the same question in different costumes: who absorbs the risk?

In AI workflows, it cannot be the agent. In government, it cannot quietly be the public when private power negotiates with itself. In cities, it may be future residents facing water or present residents priced out of housing. In markets, it is borrowers waiting on inflation and consumers watching oil. In entertainment, it is theaters, studios, states — and stunt performers who turn physical risk into practiced control.

The modern world keeps promising automation, consolidation and optimization. The unglamorous follow-up remains: fine, but who is responsible when it lands?
